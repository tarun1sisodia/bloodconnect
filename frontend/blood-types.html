Understanding Blood Types

What Are Blood Types?

Blood types are determined by the presence or absence of certain antigens – substances that can trigger an immune response – on the surface of red blood cells. There are four main blood groups: A, B, AB, and O.

Additionally, blood is classified by the Rhesus (Rh) factor, which is either present (positive) or absent (negative). The combination of these two classifications results in the eight common blood types: A+, A-, B+, B-, AB+, AB-, O+, and O-.

Why Blood Type Matching Is Important

When a person receives blood that's incompatible with their own, their immune system can reject the foreign blood cells, triggering a potentially life-threatening reaction. This is why proper blood type matching is crucial before any transfusion.

In emergency situations where there isn't time to determine blood type, O- blood (universal donor) may be used for transfusions, but this is only a temporary measure until type-specific blood becomes available.

How to Find Out Your Blood Type

There are several ways to determine your blood type:

  • Donate blood - blood donation centers will typically inform you of your blood type
  • Ask your doctor to order a blood type test
  • Check your birth certificate or military ID, which may include blood type information
  • Look at past medical records, especially if you've had surgery
  • Use an at-home blood typing kit (though these are less accurate than lab tests)

Interesting Blood Type Facts

Blood Type and Geography

Blood type distribution varies by geographic region and ethnicity. For example, B type blood is more common in Asian countries, while O is predominant in Central and South America.

Blood Type History

The ABO blood group system was discovered by Karl Landsteiner in 1901, earning him the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1930 for this groundbreaking discovery.

Beyond ABO

While ABO and Rh are the most important for transfusions, scientists have identified 38 major blood group systems with more than 600 different blood group antigens.

Blood Type Inheritance

Blood type is inherited from your parents, but doesn't follow a simple pattern. For example, two type A parents can have a type O child if both parents carry the recessive O gene.
