Blood Donation Eligibility

Learn about the general requirements and restrictions for blood donation. These guidelines help ensure the safety of both donors and recipients.

Basic Eligibility Requirements

Age

Must be at least 17 years old in most states (16 years old with parental consent in some states).

Weight

Must weigh at least 110 pounds (50 kg). Additional weight requirements may apply for donors under 18 years old.

Health

Must be in good general health and feeling well on the day of donation. No symptoms of illness, cold, or flu.

Time Between Donations

Must wait at least 56 days (8 weeks) between whole blood donations, 112 days (16 weeks) between double red cell donations, and at least 7 days between platelet donations.

Pulse and Blood Pressure

Must have normal pulse, temperature, and blood pressure on the day of donation.

Hemoglobin Levels

Must have adequate hemoglobin levels (at least 12.5 g/dL for women and 13.0 g/dL for men).

Temporary Deferrals

The following conditions may temporarily prevent you from donating blood. After the specified deferral period, you may be eligible to donate again.

Pregnancy and Childbirth

Women who are pregnant cannot donate blood. You must wait 6 weeks after giving birth before donating blood.

Recent Surgery or Serious Illness

You must be fully recovered from any surgery and off antibiotics for at least 7 days. For serious illnesses, the deferral period may vary depending on the condition and treatment.

Recent Vaccinations

Deferral periods for vaccines vary:

  • COVID-19 vaccines: No deferral for most types if you're feeling well
  • Flu shot: No deferral if you're feeling well
  • MMR, varicella, or shingles: 4-week deferral
  • Hepatitis B: No deferral

Dental Work

Minor dental work (cleaning, filling): 24-hour deferral
Major dental work (extractions, root canals): 3-day deferral
Dental surgery: 3-month deferral

Travel to Malaria-Risk Areas

If you've traveled to an area where malaria is endemic, you may be deferred for 3 months to 3 years, depending on the length of stay and other factors.

Tattoos and Piercings

Deferral periods vary by state. In states that regulate tattoo facilities, there is no deferral if the tattoo was applied in a state-regulated facility. In states that do not regulate tattoo facilities, there's a 3-month deferral. For piercings, if performed with single-use equipment, there's generally no deferral.

Permanent Deferrals

The following conditions may permanently prevent you from donating blood. These restrictions are in place to protect both the donor and recipient's health.

HIV/AIDS: Individuals who have tested positive for HIV are permanently deferred.

Hepatitis B or C: Individuals who have had hepatitis B or C are permanently deferred.

Risk of Variant Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease (vCJD): Individuals who spent a cumulative time of 3 months or more in the United Kingdom from 1980 through 1996, or 5 years or more in Europe from 1980 to the present.

Babesiosis and Chagas Disease: Individuals who have had these parasitic infections are permanently deferred.

Certain Cancer Types: Some cancers may result in permanent deferral, especially blood cancers like leukemia or lymphoma.

IV Drug Use: History of using illegal intravenous drugs.

Certain Heart and Lung Conditions: Some chronic heart and lung conditions may result in permanent deferral.

Disclaimer

The eligibility criteria provided on this page are general guidelines and may vary by blood collection center and over time as regulations change. Always check with your local blood donation center for the most current and specific eligibility requirements. The final determination of eligibility is made by medical professionals at the donation site.
